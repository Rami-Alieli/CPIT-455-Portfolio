WEEK 03–04
CLASS 01 · Domain Spine: Dependability
5 Attributes, Sources of Failure
Topics: Availability, Reliability, Safety, Security, Resilience
My Evidence (V1)

I applied the five dependability attributes to Usaruna — the e-commerce marketplace my team is building for family businesses in Saudi Arabia:

  • Availability: The platform must be online 24/7. A family seller who loses a day of visibility loses real income — this isn't a minor inconvenience, it's a livelihood issue.
  • Reliability: Orders and payments must process correctly every time. A duplicate charge or a lost order destroys the trust that the entire platform is built on.
  • Safety: The platform must prevent harmful listings — selling unsafe food products, counterfeit goods, or items that exploit buyers.
  • Security: Payment data, personal addresses, and seller information must be encrypted. We handle real SAR transactions; a breach is a legal and ethical failure.
  • Resilience: If the payment gateway (Moyasar) goes down, the system must fail gracefully — queue the transaction, alert the user, and recover without data loss.

The key insight I took from this week: dependability attributes are not features you add last — they are architectural constraints you design around from day one.

WEEK 05–06
CLASS 02 · Reliability Engineering
Fault → Error → Failure, POFOD, ROCOF, AVAIL Metrics
Topics: 8 Commandments of Reliability
My Evidence (V1)

I analyzed Usaruna's Fault → Error → Failure chain for the payment flow specifically:

  • Fault: Network timeout between Express.js backend and Moyasar API.
  • Error: Transaction state becomes ambiguous — the system doesn't know if payment was debited.
  • Failure: User is charged but order isn't created, or order is created but payment never lands.

This chain taught me why idempotency keys matter in payment engineering — if you retry a request, you must not duplicate the charge. I documented this as a required design pattern for our backend.

For reliability metrics: I estimated Usaruna needs a POFOD below 0.001 on the checkout flow (less than 1 failure per 1000 order attempts), since every failure directly costs a seller a sale.

The microservices architecture we chose (React frontend → Node.js backend → Supabase) supports independent scaling — meaning if the product listing service has high traffic, it doesn't drag down the payment service.

WEEK 07–08
CLASS 03 · Safety Engineering
Hazard Analysis, ALARP Triangle, Fault Tree Analysis
Topics: Hazard → Accident → Damage, Safety Case
My Evidence (V1)

I applied the Hazard → Accident → Damage chain to a critical scenario in Usaruna: a food product listing with missing allergen information.

  • Hazard: A seller uploads a homemade food product and omits allergen warnings (e.g., contains peanuts).
  • Accident: A customer with a peanut allergy purchases the product, unaware of the risk.
  • Damage: Severe allergic reaction. Legal liability for the platform and the seller.

My proposed protection system: Mandatory allergen declaration field for all food-category listings, with a platform-side check before publish. This breaks the hazard chain before the accident can occur.

For the ALARP analysis on Usaruna:

  • Intolerable: Payment data leak — must be designed out entirely with Moyasar tokenization + Supabase RLS.
  • ALARP Region: System downtime during peak shopping hours — mitigated with cloud redundancy but cannot be eliminated completely.
  • Acceptable: A seller receiving a slightly late payout settlement — tolerable by business standards.

WEEK 09–10
GROUP PHASE · Security & Resilience + Software Reuse
Security Engineering, Resilience Patterns, Component Reuse
Topics: Security Strategies, Reuse Plan for Usaruna
My Evidence (V1)

My individual contribution in this phase was mapping the software reuse strategy for Usaruna's architecture. I documented why each component was chosen over building from scratch:

  • Supabase Auth — I chose this over a custom JWT system because it provides battle-tested Row Level Security out of the box. Building this manually would introduce critical security vulnerabilities in our MVP.
  • Moyasar API — Saudi-localized payment gateway. Integrating this as a black-box component means we inherit their PCI compliance instead of having to achieve it ourselves.
  • React + Vite — Component-based architecture ensures UI elements (product cards, checkout flow, seller dashboard) are reusable across screens without re-writing logic.

The key engineering lesson: reuse is a reliability strategy, not laziness. Every reused component is a component that has already been tested at scale by someone else.

GROUP PHASE · Architecture & Component Engineering
Distributed Systems, Component-Based SE, Architecture Design
Topics: Ch.17 Distributed Systems, Ch.16 Component-Based SE
My Evidence (V1)

I contributed to the Architecture V1 documentation for Usaruna. My specific role was analyzing how our 3-layer architecture (Frontend → Backend API → Database) maps to distributed systems principles.

The core design decision I argued for: separating the Node.js/Express.js backend from the Supabase BaaS layer. This gives us the ability to add business logic (order state machines, pricing rules, seller verification workflows) without coupling it to the database layer.

I identified that our external integrations (Moyasar, Google Maps API, Supabase Storage) are all service-oriented components — they communicate over well-defined APIs and can be swapped or updated without affecting internal logic. This is a resilience pattern: no single external dependency can bring down the entire system.

// 03 · capstone project

🚀 Usaruna Platform — أسرنا

Usaruna | أسرنا
Empowering Family Businesses — E-commerce Marketplace for Saudi Arabia
Domain: E-Commerce Marketplace (B2C & B2B) Team: 5 Members · Iteration 1

Many family businesses in Saudi Arabia rely on informal channels like WhatsApp and Instagram to sell their products. This creates trust issues, poor order management, limited visibility, and insecure payment handling. Usaruna is a web-based marketplace connecting productive families with customers through a secure, organized, and logistically integrated platform — supporting Vision 2030's goal of empowering local entrepreneurship.

// system architecture (v1)

⚛ Frontend Layer

React.js & Vite with Tailwind CSS for responsive design. Component-based architecture ensures UI elements (product cards, checkout flow, seller dashboard) are reusable across screens without duplicating logic.

⚙ Backend Layer

Node.js with Express.js handles business logic routing and external API orchestration. Decoupled from the database layer to allow independent scaling and state machine logic for orders.

🗄 Database Layer

Supabase (PostgreSQL) as a BaaS — provides Database, secure Authentication, and Row Level Security. Each user role (buyer, seller, admin) has enforced data access boundaries.

🔗 External Services

Moyasar (payments), Supabase Storage (product images), Google Maps API (delivery routing). All service-oriented components — replaceable without affecting internal business logic.

// software reuse strategy

React & Vite

Core SPA framework — eliminates full-page reloads, improves UX for marketplace browsing.

Tailwind CSS

Utility-first framework replacing manual CSS — cuts UI development time significantly.

Supabase Auth

Battle-tested authentication with RLS — avoids the security pitfalls of custom JWT implementation.

Supabase Storage

Secure object storage for product images — scalable without managing our own file server.

Moyasar API

Saudi-localized payment gateway — inherits their PCI compliance instead of achieving it ourselves.

Google Maps API

Delivery routing and address geocoding — precise logistics without building routing algorithms.

// alarp risk model

🚫 Unacceptable Risk

Payment data leak — unauthorized access to buyer payment information or seller banking details.

Mitigation: Strict tokenization via Moyasar + Supabase RLS policies. Must be designed out entirely.

⚖ ALARP Region

System downtime & delivery delays — acceptable with conditions during peak traffic periods.

Mitigation: Smart delivery routing algorithms + reliable cloud hosting. Cannot be eliminated, but must be minimized.

✅ Acceptable Risk

Fake or unfair reviews — a seller receiving an unjustified negative rating from a bad-faith buyer.

Mitigation: Only verified buyers who completed a purchase can leave reviews. Risk is tolerable by platform standards.

👤 My Individual Role in Iteration 1

In the V1 phase of Usaruna, my primary contribution was designing the Fault Tree Analysis for the Order Completion Failure scenario and documenting the software reuse rationale for the team. I identified that the two root causes of order failure (payment gateway downtime and database connection loss) are independent — meaning an OR gate is correct in the fault tree, and each must be mitigated independently. I also argued for the architectural decision to separate the Node.js backend from Supabase, which gives us the ability to add payment retry logic without touching the database schema. This V1 documentation forms my individual engineering proof for Iteration 1.

❓ What changed in how I think?

"Before HIMMA, I thought software engineering meant writing code that works. Now I understand it means building systems that can be trusted — and trust requires proof, not just functionality. Every design decision I make now starts with the question: 'How would I defend this to someone whose safety depends on it?'"

📈 My biggest V1 insight

"Working on Usaruna's FTA, I realized that listing five dependability attributes is trivial — applying them to a real system with real trade-offs is where engineering actually happens. You can't have infinite availability AND infinite security without infinite budget. You have to choose, justify, and document."

⚖ Safety vs Reliability

"A system can be perfectly reliable — executing its specification 100% of the time — and still cause harm if the specification itself is wrong. Safety is about what the system should never do, regardless of the spec. A payment system that reliably charges the wrong amount is reliable but not safe."

💼 What I carry into my career

"The burden of proof is on the engineer. In safety engineering, you are guilty until proven innocent — you must demonstrate your system won't fail, not just hope that it won't. I will never ship a critical feature without a documented risk analysis. HIMMA gave me that standard."
